ICLab Research Consent Form

Characterizing worldwide deployments of Internet censorship technology

INVESTIGATORS

Project Title: Internet Censorship Lab (ICLab)
Principal Investigators: Prof. Ronald J. Deibert (Munk School of Global Affairs, University of Toronto), Prof. Phillipa Gill (Stony Brook University, Stony Brook, NY, USA)
Sponsor: National Science Foundation (USA)
You are being asked to be a volunteer in a research study that seeks to characterize worldwide deployments of Internet censorship technology. This phase of the project is a pilot to test our censorship detection platform and receive feedback to improve the system. This research project is led by researchers at University of Toronto and Stony Brook University.

PURPOSE OF THIS STUDY

The purpose of this study is: To document and analyze the practice of national-level Internet censorship through the creation of a global network of researchers running censorship measurement software.

This measurement software will run tests from your computer or dedicated hardware device which attempt to access a list of sensitive URLs. These sensitive URLs include political, social and religious content, and can include such material as oppositional political content, pornography and content critical of religion. The software will collect data from these tests and send it back to servers hosted at the University of Toronto which are maintained by the researchers. This data will be used to determine what content is being censored, what technical methods are used for censorship and how censorship changes over time. This is a pilot phase of the project that is intended to test the measurement software and collect user feedback that can be used to improve the system and research protocol.

STUDY REQUIREMENTS

If you decide to be in this study, you will help run network measurement experiment software from network locations you have access to. You will be asked to run the software from either a dedicated hardware device that the researchers will supply to you or from your personal computer.

Specific participation requirements (personal computer):

Downloading a software client and installing it on to your computer. This should take approximately 5 minutes and only needs to be done once.

Running the software on your computer. The software will run as a background process on your computer. It will run tests for network censorship which may take up to one hour, but do not require your interaction.

Providing feedback on the software and testing process when requested by the research team

Specific participation requirements (dedicated hardware):

Providing an Internet connection for the dedicated hardware device and ensuring the device has connectivity.

Provided feedback on the testing process when requested by the research team.

On average it is expected that participation will require 1-3 hours a week over the course of 12 months.

STUDY BENEFITS

Throughout the 12 month duration of the project you will receive analysis reports of the measurement experiments you have participated in. These reports may help you gain a better understanding of the prevalence, depth, and breadth of Internet filtering in your country.

There are no other direct benefits from participation in this research study, although your participation may help contribute to the public good by educating society about the prevalence, depth, and breadth of Internet filtering around the world.

SECURITY and CONFIDENTIALITY

Every effort will be made to maintain the strictest confidentiality. Data will only be used for the purposes outlined in the information and consent forms. Only members of the research team will have access to personally identifiable data. No information about research participants, or provided by research participants during the research, will be disclosed to others without the participants’ prior formal written consent.

Electronic data will be stored in a secure server environment maintained at the University of Toronto. Any personally identifiable data kept on computers outside of this secure server environment will be encrypted at all times using strong encryption (128 bit AES) except to the extent researchers need to decrypt them during use.

Experiment data may include issuing requests for URLs found in the URL list (similar to what a user does manually with a Web browser, such as Firefox, but automated), DNS queries (mapping hostnames such as Google.com to IP address such as 72.14.52.3), or traceroutes (measuring network paths). Experiments may log network traffic associated with the experiment (e.g.,network packets and timestamps). The software will also contain a unique user ID to allow us to map users back to their informed consent and identify faulty installation.

Once an experiment has completed the resulting data will be sent, along with the experiment ID and user ID to the data management system. Once the data management system confirms receipt of the data it will be removed from the client software. Personally identifiable data that will be collected and transmitted will be your IP address. Experiment results will be encrypted to ensure they are not observable in transit and this personally identifiable data (as well as the unique user ID) will only be accessible to researchers at the University of Toronto.

Whenever the client communicates with the experiment controller or the data management system the data will be encrypted and may also be sent to a proxy (e.g., in Amazon EC2 data centers) before being forwarded on to the target system. The proxy will not store the data and will not contain the key required to decrypt any data it forwards.

RISKS

The following risks may occur as a result of you being in this study:

Your ISP or network administrator may be able to see the list of websites being accessed by the measurement software if they are monitoring the network. There is a risk that accessing such websites and running these tests violates network policies.

The potential risks posed by conducted these tests is highly specific to the context in which the tests take place. These risks are highly dependent on the domestic political and legal context in your country.
